DFM FEA Kinetic Energy

15 lb Combat Robot: Master Oogway

Cost Optimization & High-KE Weapon Design

The Challenge & Objective

Our team faced a critical gap: our 15 lb combat robot design lacked a high-Kinetic Energy (KE) weapon system that could be manufactured within our strict budget and weight limits. The purpose of this project was to engineer a weapon that maximized destructive potential while minimizing fabrication complexity.

"Design a cost-optimized, high-KE vertical spinner that adheres to the 15 lb weight limit and reduces manufacturing expenditure by at least 25%."

Design Process & Implementation

Iterative Optimization for DFM

Initial analysis identified a critical manufacturing bottleneck: the original weapon geometry was too thick for our vendor to cost-effectively waterjet the center mounting hole. This constraint threatened to exceed our budget with expensive CNC machining costs.

Design Evolution: Transitioning from a thick, costly geometry to a thinner, wider profile optimized for waterjet cutting.

I executed an iterative redesign (shown above), modifying the AR500 steel disks to be thinner while increasing their outer diameter. This strategic adjustment successfully maintained the exact target Moment of Inertia (MoI) and mass required for the weapon's energy output, all while making the part compatible with high-volume, cost-efficient waterjet cutting.

Strategic Manufacturing Decisions

To connect these new disks, I designed a custom 6061 Aluminum hub. A key cost-saving measure was the decision to manually tap the bolt holes on the hub rather than specifying a CNC threading operation. This decision alone, combined with the optimized disk geometry, directly contributed to a 31% reduction in overall manufacturing costs without compromising structural integrity.

Turnbuckle Differential Mount & Tensioner

Mechanical Design Project, UC Berkeley

SolidWorks FEA Topology Optimization Force Analysis

Problem

Standard differential mounts lack integrated, precise chain tensioning systems, forcing the use of separate, often cumbersome hardware.

Purpose

To engineer a consolidated differential mount that incorporates a robust, adjustable chain tensioner and a jacking bar interface.

Objective

Design, optimize, and validate the integrated system using FEA, ensuring all components meet a minimum Factor of Safety (FoS) of 1.5.

Design Process & Implementation

A. Force Analysis & Mechanism Selection

  • Performed manual force analysis to establish baseline load conditions, accounting for chain wrap angle and taking moments along the differential.
  • Engineered the mount to function as a bell crank mechanism, where adjusting a turnbuckle provides precise, measurable tension to the chain.

Image Placeholder: Bell Crank & Turnbuckle Mechanism

Diagram showing the force vectors and tensioning concept.

B. Component Optimization & Validation

  • Designed custom turnbuckles using hex pipe and threaded rods to achieve greater strength and finer adjustability than off-the-shelf parts.
  • Conducted topology optimization studies on the mount and jacking bar to systematically remove non-critical material, minimizing weight.
  • Validated the final optimized geometry through comprehensive FEA in SolidWorks, ensuring all components met the minimum FoS of 1.5 under all load cases.

The topology optimization and bell crank design were highly successful. The final mounts were reduced to 115g and 150g, and the associated jacking bar's weight was reduced by 50%. All components, including tabs and mounting hardware, were validated in FEA to meet the strict 1.5 Factor of Safety requirement.

AI Rover Wrist Mechanism

Mechanical Engineer, Space Technologies at Berkeley

SolidWorks FEA Robotics

CAD Model Rendering Placeholder

Pulley-Driven Differential Assembly

Simulated 3D Model View of the Multi-Axis Wrist

Overview and Design

I designed and prototyped the robotic arm's wrist mechanism for the AI Rover. The primary goal was achieving multi-axis control with high precision while critically minimizing mass for mission payload efficiency.

Key Mechanism: Pulley-Driven Differential

The solution implemented was a pulley-driven differential system. This ingenious design utilizes a single motor input to differentially control two independent axes (pitch and roll), significantly reducing the number of heavy components (motors and wiring) needed near the end-effector.

Structural Optimization: 20% Mass Reduction

  • Conducted extensive structural optimization using SolidWorks and FEA to identify and remove non-load-bearing material from the housing and links.
  • The iterative redesign process resulted in an overall 20% mass reduction of the final component, directly contributing to increased rover endurance and payload capacity.
  • Delivered formal design presentations and maintained comprehensive technical documentation for manufacturing.

Self-Driving Car Simulation

Independent Project, UC Berkeley

JavaScript HTML5 Canvas Neural Networks

Self-Driving Simulation Visualization

Image URL Placeholder

Visualization of the JavaScript physics and neural network decision making

Technical Execution

This project involved building a fully contained autonomous driving simulation from the ground up, using only JavaScript and HTML5 Canvas. The focus was on optimizing performance and creating a highly realistic physics and interaction environment for the learning agent.

Custom Feedforward Neural Network

  • Developed a feedforward neural network from scratch without external libraries, directly implementing matrix operations for weight and bias adjustments.
  • Leveraged 1000+ parallelized computations (using modern browser APIs) to accelerate the training process by 5x, enabling high-speed, real-time decision-making in under 100ms per frame.

Physics and Traffic Engine

  • Programmed a robust physics engine to handle realistic vehicle dynamics, including acceleration, friction, and steering.
  • Implemented collision detection and dynamic traffic generation logic to manage 50 concurrent vehicles, creating a lifelike road interaction environment.

How It Works

1. Physics, Collisions, and Car Types

The simulation runs on a custom physics engine. Each car is defined by a precise polygon, allowing for accurate collision detection using a separating axis theorem (SAT) implementation. The three types of cars create a dynamic environment:

  • DUMMY Cars: Act as moving obstacles, traveling in straight lines.
  • KEYS Car: A single car I can control with the arrow keys for debugging and testing.
  • AI Cars: Self-driving agents controlled by the neural network.

Image Placeholder: Car Collision Polygons

Diagram showing the car models and their collision shapes.

2. Sensors & Inputs

Each AI car is equipped with five forward-facing sensors, implemented as rays. These rays detect the distance to two types of objects:

  • Road Borders
  • Other "DUMMY" cars (obstacles)

The sensors return a reading as an offset (a value between 0 and 1, where 1 is no object and 0 is object right in front). These five offset values become the inputs for the neural network.

Image Placeholder: Sensor Ray Diagram

Visualization of the 5 rays projecting from the car.

3. Neural Network Architecture

The car's "brain" is a feedforward neural network with a **5-6-4 architecture:**

  • 5 Input Neurons: One for each sensor ray.
  • 6 Hidden Neurons: A single hidden layer to process the inputs.
  • 4 Output Neurons: Representing `Forward`, `Left`, `Right`, and `Reverse`.

The network processes information by taking all 5 sensor inputs, multiplying them by their corresponding weights, and summing them at the hidden layer. A function then determines if each hidden neuron's value is 1 or 0 (active or not). This process is repeated from the hidden layer to the output layer. The result is a set of binary outputs (1 or 0) for each of the 4 controls, simulating a key press or release.

Image Placeholder: Neural Network Diagram (5-6-4)

Visual showing the inputs, hidden layer, and output nodes.

4. Neuroevolution & Learning

The AI learns to drive through a process of neuroevolution, or "survival of the fittest."

  1. Generate Population: The simulation generates N cars (e.g., 100) with randomized neural network weights and biases.
  2. Evaluate Fitness: All cars drive simultaneously. The "fitness" of each car is measured by how far it travels before crashing.
  3. Select Best: After a set time, the simulation saves the car that traveled the furthest as the "Best Car." Its weights and biases are stored.
  4. Mutate & Repeat: A new generation of N cars is created. This time, instead of being fully random, each new car is a copy of the "Best Car" with a small, random mutation applied to its weights.

This process repeats, and over many generations, the mutations that lead to better driving (avoiding objects, staying on the road) are naturally selected for. Eventually, the simulation "evolves" an optimal car that can navigate the track indefinitely.

Lightweight Mechanism Design for Wearable Robotics

This research project involved the mechanical design and simulation of joint mechanisms for an upper-limb exoskeleton. The primary objective was to reduce the overall weight of the apparatus while maintaining high rigidity and structural reliability to assist users.

Comprehensive FEA with ANSYS

  • Designed and iterated multiple 3D-printed joint prototypes in SolidWorks, focusing on kinematic and dynamic constraints.
  • Conducted over 12 detailed stress and deformation simulations in ANSYS under various loading conditions (e.g., lifting, static hold).

Achieving Weight Reduction

  • Utilized topology optimization techniques guided by FEA results to refine the geometry of the load-bearing components.
  • Successfully reduced the weight of the critical joint mechanisms by 13% without compromising the Factor of Safety, critical for long-term user comfort and battery life.
